I almost hooked up with a werewolf… but before we got to third base, he dragged me off to jail.

Apparently, he's the leader of the pack in town.

The fact that I’m an illegal blood wolf—a vampire/werewolf hybrid—doesn’t help my case.

And he didn't appreciate me accidentally biting him when we were kissing, either.

Luckily, or unluckily, he seems to think I'm his true mate, which means he won't let anyone hurt me.

But he also won't let me go.

Between his possessiveness, my need to be near him, and the vampire clan hunting me, one thing's becoming clear:

I won't be able to avoid my werewolf forever.

This is a steamy, fun, full-length standalone romance with a happily ever after!

This was surprisingly good. Admittedly, the world-building is a little weak, and as mentioned in the author's note, the existence of the blood wolves left something to be desired. (Why would a vampire create another vampire, crossed with a shifter, to create a delicacy? Why is that not some sort of cannibalism? Then again, in this world, vampire mates can only feed from each other, which seems like a sort of weird recycled two-girls-one-cup style of nutrient to me.)

But this book had something that a lot of romance authors tend to leave out: romance. I mean, yeah, we all get the smexy times in romance novels, but anyone who read more than a few knows that the storylines tend to be meet, fight, kiss, fight, kiss, kiss, fight, shag, fight, fight, fight, SHAAAAAG, HEA. The characters usually feel like cardboard cutouts of each other, especially the men, and there isn't a lot of on-page "getting to know you" outside of sharing traumatic backstories that make alpholes feel human and doormats feel sympathetic.

And yeah, there was some of that here. I even understand why authors tend to "yada yada" over getting-to-know you scenes. But in this book, what I really liked is that the author didn't do that -- she allowed them to get to know each other as people. And it isn't all verbal, although there were some of those too. For example, rather than simply buying her a new designer wardrobe, all "billionaire" style, Madd notes her quirky style based on one meeting together, buys shoes similar to what she was wearing, but without holes, buys dresses similar to what she was wearing when they met (again, but in better shape), notes without her telling him that she's self-conscious about her hair looking a certain quirky way, how she likes to smell, that she likes makeup and what kind... that sort of thing. He thinks about it. He takes her tastes into account. That is so much sweeter and romantic to me than buying an entire wardrobe of designer dresses that cost more than my yearly salary. It gave me a bit of the same vibe that made me pay attention to Cate C. Wells even when the first book I read by her didn't really "hit" with me (and honestly, several since haven't, but I'd still classify myself as a "fan" of that author and hope to do the same with this one).

And sure, yeah, this is a werewolf mate novel, so there's plenty of alpha you-will-be-mine stuff, but it didn't feel so arbitrary. By the end of the book, it actually made sense on the page that these two liked each other, brought out the best in each other, made each other laugh. And yeah, by the end it did evolve (or devolve) into pornier territory, and there were plenty of arguments along the way, but in the meantime, it actually felt like two people getting to know each other.

So why did this not rate higher? 1) The first half of the book (ish) happened in 24 hours and it just seemed like too much, too fast. 2) Despite how very much I enjoyed it, I'm not sure it's one I'd check out from KU again, or buy, for a re-read. 3) There wasn't much tension from the danger Love and Tori believed they were in and any that might have been happened off page, so we don't even really see the comeuppance for their torturers. 4) Love has a slight Not-Like-Other-Girls vibe to her. 5) I'm not entirely sure.

I do know I'll absolutely be looking for more written by Lola Glass, although I might skip the other two books in this series and look at some of her others instead.

All this book is is the FMC being pampered and taken care of (in all ways) really.
But that is arguable not necessarily a flaw. It's what the book wanted to be so its more a me-thing I guess.

My more objective criticism is that the entire premise and the title of the book itself are just false.

The explanations and reasons for why the MC acts the way she does in terms of trying to avoid her werewolf are just utterly unbelievable and feel completely out of place.
It's the only real conflict that is going on and its completely contrived and makes no sense.
The book never even tries to come up with believable reasons for the whole avoidance game and every time the author brings it up it comes completely out of left field and distrupts immersion.

It feels like the author just wrote a generic feel-good urban romantasy but every now and then remembered that she named the book "the art of avoiding ..." and had to add some avoiding into it at random times.
