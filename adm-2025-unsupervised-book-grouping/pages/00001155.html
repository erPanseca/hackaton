Set during the dark days of World War II, Agatha Christie’s N or M? puts two most unlikely espionage agents, Tommy and Tuppence Beresford, on the trail of a pair of Nazi spies who have murdered Britain’s top agent.

World War II is raging, and while the RAF struggles to keep the Luftwaffe at bay, Britain faces a sinister threat from “the enemy within”—Nazis posing as ordinary citizens.

With pressure mounting, the intelligence service appoints two improbable spies, Tommy and Tuppence Beresford. Their mission: to seek out a man and a woman from among the colorful guests at Sans Souci, a seaside hotel. But this assignment is far from an easy stroll along the promenade—N and M have just murdered Britain’s finest agent and no one can be trusted...

Best couple in the Christieverse.



At least 50% of the time, I'm disappointed with the way Agatha wrote couples.
The worst offenders for me were a woman who happily gave up her successful business to be married, and a woman who felt her heart flutter with love when her man proved how much he cared by trying to strangle her.
However, in N or M Christie nailed it.
Tommy's respect for Tuppence is pretty amazing in that he doesn't expect her to sit at home docilely, and he's quite proud to have her working with him. And Tuppence is so sure of herself and secure in who she and Tommy are together that she doesn't do some of the silly things you see women in older books do. <--fainting, screaming, flittering about making things worse, etc.



So it's WWII, and Tommy and Tuppence are being told they are too old to do anything to help with the war effort. Which is really funny because they're only in their late 40s early 50s. I think retirement age is what...85 or 90 now? Times have certainly changed.
Anyway spoilery things happen, they eventually end up working undercover to try and ferret out two of the top German spies out there. Known only as N and M, they were traced to a small town by a British operative who was killed before he could report his full suspicions.

Thing is, the actual spy stuff was somewhat dull. I didn't care whodunnit. The parts I was most interested in were Tommy & Tuppence themselves and the interactions with their grown children. The children who have no idea that their mom and dad are cool spies and think that they are both tucked away in the country somewhere safe.



Oh! And this has nothing to do with the story, but I ran across these hideous limited edition Tommy & Tuppence figurines online and just had to share.



I haven't read all of the Tommy & Tuppence books so I don't have a favorite yet, but this one was fun. I'm not sure if I'd go around recommending all of them willy-nilly to someone who is just wanting a good book, but N or M one might be an outlier that anyone looking for a cozy spy mystery might enjoy.
Recommended for Christie Mystery Fans.

Title: N or M?
Author: Agatha Christie
Genre: Mystery, Espionage
Publication Year: 1941

Review: Book #3 of the Tommy and Tuppence mystery series by Agatha Christie is a unique novel. For one thing, it works as a mystery novel, but it also works as an espionage story. Tommy and Tuppence are now middle-aged, empty-nesters that have grown children, veterans of the first World War, looking to participate in the second. Tommy has been recruited to go undercover as a widower to find traitorous Brits willing to behave as fifth column sympathizers for the Germans. He is told not to communicate with his wife, which he obeys, but Tuppence creates an undercover dossier for herself. She has never been one to play the role of an idle spouse.

Obviously, the stakes for this murder mystery, espionage tale are doubled due to the international and voluntary peril undergone by the witty and glib couple. In my opinion, the fast pace, the suspense, and faithful characterization makes this the best book in the series. I give the novel five stars, not because it is a perfect tale. It isn't, but it demonstrates a perfect understanding of a genre mashup that works exceptionally well as mystery with red herrings galore, and espionage with multinational intrigue and cloak-dagger suspense.

***2018 Summer of Spies***

A fast, fun expedition into espionage! I liked the fact that Christie allowed her main characters to age and change a bit. As is true for most of us, they retained their basic characteristics which turned them into spy hunters to begin with, but they are dealing with details that afflict us all as we age. Younger folk (including the Beresford children) no longer see our relevance and no one wants the skills that we have on offer—just ask anyone who is over 40 and unemployed and they will tell you all about it!

Tommy must have a thick skull, because he once again gets clonked on the head in this novel, but manages to come out of things un-addled. The plot is not as smooth as Christie’s murder mysteries, but it is fun to see Tuppence trying to pretend to be a middle-aged lady who knits & gossips. Tommy at least gets to go out and play golf.

I’m currently reading a biography of Ian Fleming and I’m at the point of reading about his experiences in WWI in the Royal Navy’s Intelligence Unit—although this T&T caper seems a bit outlandish, it’s not that far off of some of the imaginative schemes that NID came up with to try to thwart the Nazis, which surprised me a lot. All of the global intelligence services have come a long way since the 1940s.

A bit of summer fun.

As light reading between more demanding literary works, Agatha Christie never fails. Although Tommy and Tuppence were always my least favourite sleuths of Agatha Christie’s, the detective formula largely follows the same formulas as in the Hercule Poirot and Miss Marple stories.

This one has a bit of Fawlty Towers about it. It’s on the eve of World War II, and T & T go sleuthing in a sleepy seaside boarding house. There they meet the usual suspects: a retired major, a strange foreign woman, a dimwitted elderly woman and her hypochondriac husband, a seemingly devoted mother and her child, a suspicious young German, etc. Will T & & be able to unravel the spies of the ‘fifth column’ before Hitler invades Britain, or will they be found out …?

Although the hotel boasts of no Basil Fawlty, the story is like a comedy of manners here and there, with some delightful phrases and characterizations. The hypochondriac coughs aggressively, the little child goes into ecstasies of mirth, the imbecile wife murmurs pacifically and so on. It got a bit soppy toward the end and was, in essence, predictably unpredictable.

My tattered copy is a testament to the days when I first began my English book collection, searching the dusty bookshelves in second-hand shops up and down Charing Cross Road. I first read this book back then but remember not a single thing. So, less demanding may also mean less rewarding, but there’s something to be said for a bit of brain candy before (and after) more taxing reading experiences. Dostoevsky, here I come!

This novel sees the detective duo of Tommy and Tuppence in the midst of World War-2. N and M are the initials given to two of Hitler's agents whom Tommy and Tuppence hunt for. A character in the novel Major Bletchley is a former Indian army officer who claims to know the secrets of Britain's wartime efforts. During World War-2, the code breakers at Bletchley Park in Buckinghamshire under Dilly Knox had broken German Enigma machine cyphers giving British military to Germany’s secret plans. "Trouble with us in this country is that we're so absurdly unsuspicious."
In one of my previous AC reviews, I mentioned an article from TimeOut which I'll now link here:
https://www.timeout.com/newyork/news/...

The writer of the article (Adam Feldman) compiled a list, during the rage of COVID, in which he evaluated all of Christie's work related to crime. Other readers with an AC interest may basically find the article helpful - but why am I posting it here? 

The main reason has to do with the 'Tommy & Tuppence' series (of 5 novels) - assessed generally as 'not good'; or as Feldman writes, "You can safely avoid reading any of these books." (Ouch!)

'N or M?' is the first (of the 5) that I've read... and I actually found it to be quite good. ~ quite different from Poirot / Miss Marple / and other, standalone novels. ~ yet still quite recognizable as a Christie book (with its standard use of multiple suspects).

Published just before the US entered WWII, the focus is the palpable German threat of UK infiltration. Perhaps Christie was enticed by the thought of her own take on the work of her contemporary Eric Ambler, whose thrillers began appearing in 1936. Thrillers can come with a challenge found perhaps less often in murder mysteries: a particularly diabolical element of growing menace - which is slowly, then effectively enhanced in 'N or M?' 

This becomes especially chilling when Christie reminds us of certain particulars of enemy agent operation:
To know when to use the truth was the essence of successful deception.
I should also mention that Tommy & Tuppence are very engaging characters, quite refreshing as a married couple: sort of Christie's sans-alcohol take on Hammett's Nick & Nora Charles (who had appeared in 'The Thin Man' in 1934). 

'N or M?' is not exactly the 'lighthearted' thriller that some consider it to be. In its own way, it's fairly nerve-wracking. Not as moody as what John le Carré would eventually bring to the genre, but well-crafted in terms of suspense, logic and twists; tempered with a whisper of humor. It quite accomplishes what it sets out to do (keeping in mind that what's primary is narrative skill, along with a certain 'entertainment' factor).

In a subsequent reread of Feldman's article, I found many instances in which my opinion differed - and that's fine; what works for some will not work as well for others. He did appropriately designate what's impossible to dispute as some of Christie's best work (titles like 'And Then There Were None', 'The Murder of Roger Ackroyd') but he also gave higher rankings for some that I would place lower (and vice versa). 

My point is that readers need to trust their guts (and instincts) - much in the way that they give trust to a writer they have enjoyed - when said writer is stretching him / herself. (I read all of Philip K. Dick's SF novels. I didn't enjoy them all equally but I'm glad I stuck with him - but that's what we do when we feel kinship. And I feel a kinship with AC.) 

In short: take reading opinions - even mine - with a grain of salt. You don't have to finish every book. But you can certainly start the ones you're drawn to for whatever reason and take it from there. The very-valued element of surprise may very well be your own!
