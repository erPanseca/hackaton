Pierce Hunt #1

Hunt Them Down

Simon Gervais

4.27

Rate this book
The dark world he’s been fighting against has caught up with him. Will his daughter pay the price?

Former Army Ranger Pierce Hunt is no stranger to violence. Fresh off a six-month suspension, he’s itching to hit a notorious Mexican drug cartel where it hurts, even if that means protecting crime boss Vicente Garcia, a witness in the case against sadistic cartel leader Valentina Mieles. But things spiral out of control when the cartel murders Garcia and kidnaps his granddaughter and an innocent bystander, Hunt’s own teenage daughter.

Mieles wants the new head of the Garcia family on a plate—literally. Hunt has seventy-two hours to deliver, or Mieles will execute the girls live on social media. With the clock ticking, Hunt goes off the grid and teams up with Garcia’s daughter, a former lover and current enemy. To save the girls, Hunt will have to become a man he swore he’d never be again: an avenging killer without limits or mercy.

(As of 12/1/18 -- If you are interested in this book, be aware there are some glitches in the release date with Amazon. Hopefully they will have it fixed today...I was able to receive a Kindle edition through Amazon Prime)

The author lost me early on when he wove a plot that I felt was extremely contrived.
We are supposed to believe that a DEA operation designed to take down a major part of a drug cartel would take a reporter along with them. Even if that is believed, no one patted the reporter down to check for a cell phone (to prevent the reporter from doing what he did, tweet about the operation beforehand and alert his millions of followers, as well as the drug operation about to be busted). The author’s own words state “This was a busier neighborhood than he would have liked," yet the action has several DEA officers firing into a crowded street at the fleeing perpetrators. Not only were there innocent civilians around, but the perpetrators were driving away. Basically, the lives of civilians were greatly endangered and firing at people who are escaping – moving away from the agents – make it very difficult to believe that the person running the operation would have authorized agents to shoot.

However, the story would not have been able to proceed in the direction the author wanted it to take, which is why I state that the plot was contrived. While it could happen, the potential is quite limited, and following it up by placing a loaded gun against a civilian’s head (and not getting fired for that) placed more of a strain on my credibility meter than I could take.

The characterization is typical: divorced from his wife because of the job, a difficult relationship with his daughter. Agent Hunt carries around the same baggage we see in countless other books. Other plot lines are taken straight out of the newspapers, opioids kill tens of thousands (once you add in the illegal opioids), guns kill people, the depiction of the typical law enforcement person in the United States as crooked, uncaring, and ruthless. After a while, everything begins to become one big cliché.

As I said, the author lost me early on, as everything I have described happened in the first 10% of the book.

The hard-to-believe plot continued as I doggedly continued to read. The author does provide good description, and keeps the characters to a minimum, making them easy to follow from chapter to chapter. The main character jumps from one action-oriented event into the next, making this a great book for adrenaline junkies but not for anyone seeking a book with a realistic story. Thus, some people will like it and others won’t. I’ve shared just enough for the most readers to decide whether or not this is the book for you. Three stars.

Very much a stand-alone book, it also leaves you wanting to follow the further adventures of Pierce Hunt. If the next book is this good, Hunt could become one of my new favourites.

Hunt Them Down is a well-crafted and well-written action thriller that swiftly introduces a new badass protagonist to be remembered by the readers. The first chapter itself, involving a DEA bust gone violent, establishes the gritty and action-paced tone of the book and gives a great introduction of Pierce Hunt, a straight shooter DEA agent, team-leader, and an Army Ranger (I have often heard “Once A Ranger Always A Ranger”, so cannot say former Ranger).

The narrative follows Pierce Hunt, on his first day back to work in Miami after the violent turn of events taking place in the introduction, where Hunt is asked to help an investigation against The Black Tosca, a feared leader of her drug cartel. Hunt is asked to help out due to the witness being a criminal Pierce put away during his undercover mission with the DEA. The backstory behind the undercover stint adds to the story an emotional layer due to Hunt falling in love with a woman undercover while having a family back home. With the news of an imminent betrayal reaching The Black Tosca, she tasks her trained operators to not only put an end to the treachery, but to exact vengeance for a horrific tragedy she experienced at a very young age. This sets in a motion a violent and brutal chain of events in which Hunt’s young daughter is taken by the cartel members, and it is up-to Hunt to get her back in one piece.

What makes Pierce Hunt so memorable for me is grounded characterization. He isn’t perfect. He has made mistakes, and he makes some more in the events of the book. It is his determination and perseverance to stand tall in the face of adversity and embracing his emotions to fuel his determination that resonated with me. It also helps that Hunt is a bona-fide badass extremely lethal with an MP5 as well as with a Glock 22.

The narrative is well-crafted with relatable and realistic characters that balance the fast-paced and action-packed vibe of the book with strong emotions and dilemmas that make for such a fascinating read. Each character brings emotional baggage to the story and acts accordingly, creating an intriguing tale of flawed human beings. Both good guys and gals as well as bad guys and gals are imbued with a cognizant emotional center that adds to the tension. The characters are not good or bad for the sake of it. The motivations are well defined.

The action sequences are some of the finest I have read. Each gunshot in the shootouts is easily visualizable for the readers with accurate tactical descriptions of the weapons and the equipment used. The action sequences, shootouts and hand-to-hand combat, felt cinematic in nature with respect to the attention to detail and descriptions to help readers visualize the nuances of the combat.

There is no dull moment in this thriller. From starting to the very ending, the book captures the attention with amazing action, well-defined protagonists and antagonists that you can root for and hate respectively, captivating storyline comprising of tense and emotional moments in the midst of brutal action and race-against-time thrills. The ending presents a cliff-hanger of sorts that will get reader super excited to want to pick up the next book in the series immediately. Pierce Hunt has quickly become one of my favorite characters in the genre and I look forward to reading more of him. This is a must-read action thriller for the action-thriller aficionados.

When you take out the specifics, this book has an intriguing premise: an unhinged DEA agent’s daughter gets kidnapped, and he has to team up with his biggest rival, the head of a drug cartel, to get her back.

I can see what the author was going for here: enemies have to work together, an agent has to betray the laws he upholds, etc etc. But take a step back and appreciate just how profoundly absurd this premise is:

The DEA agent, Hunt, went undercover for two years, befriending the new head of a cartel, Tony, and seducing Tony’s sister, Ana (falling in love with her and screwing up his marriage). After he testifies against Ana’s cartel, she obviously hates him and swears revenge.

But Tony’s daughter is also best friends with Hunt’s daughter. This isn’t because of Hunt’s undercover assignment, it’s a complete coincidence— and neither Hunt nor anybody in the cartel even realize the connection until both girls get kidnapped. That’s not all— the bigger cartel that kidnaps the girls hires Hunt’s old army buddy to kill him. At this point, we’re just pulling plot points out of a hat at random.

I’m a big fan of radical plot twists but there’s radical and then there’s completely unbelievable.

It doesn’t help that Hunt is written like Michael Scarn in the Office… that is, like a juvenile power fantasy of what a law enforcement officer would be. Hunt is hyper-competent and intimidating to the point of absurdity (I lost count of how many times his foes literally pissed or shit their pants when facing him). What seems like it should be a dark streak of law-breaking and dangerous behavior gets completely undercut because the author (an ex-cop) is such a Thin Blue Line cheerleader that he can’t bear to actually condemn Hunt’s actions. ANY of them. Even when Hunt puts a pistol to the head of an unarmed journalist and threatens to kill him, it’s because the journalist is an asshole and deserves it.

This kind of copaganda happens throughout the book. If a cop has a chance to shoot somebody and hesitates, they die. Alternatively, if they DO shoot somebody, it’s always the right call and should never be questioned. Anything a cop does is morally acceptable because all cops are beyond reproach, and when they break the law, they do so because the law is wrong. Here’s actual dialogue between Hunt and one of his cop buddies:

Hunt: “I’ve got a favor to ask.”
Other cop: “You got it.”
Hunt: “It could be dangerous and illegal too.”
Other cop: “But is it honorable and will it serve justice?”

This is not just ends justifying means, it’s GOALS justifying means, and it’s honestly a chilling insight into how cops see themselves as above the law and beyond criticism.

Importantly, this twisted morality also undermines the premise of the book. The fun of a plot like this is that when the cop has to break the law to get his daughter back, he has to go against not only his own morals, but his buddies back on the force who are, you know, still trying to enforce the law. The rogue cop finds himself stripped of his usual resources and has to come up with creative solutions, while dodging the innocent agents he’s essentially betraying.

Not so in this book. Because cops are perfect in this world, Hunt never questions his own actions. He never has a moral crisis. Furthermore, Hunt’s cop buddies unquestionably side with Hunt. They don’t try to stop him in any way, they even give themselves up and let him incapacitate them. A lot of them, like in the convo above, actively help him, so he’s not even stripped of resources.

It’s not just gross this way, it’s boring.

Simon Gervais has a resume that reads like many protagonists in your favorite novels, starting his career off as a junior infantry officer, he received extensive training in advanced recon and small-unit tactics. Once his service to the military was complete, he went on to use that specialized training as a federal agent, serving as a drug investigator, then rising through the ranks and with that gaining more experience, he was selected to be part of an anti-terrorism unit. With this unit he deployed to various countries and collaborated with local teams to hunt terrorists from such places as; Israel, The United Kingdom, Germany and France, to name a few.

Simon has written three novels and one short novella in his international best-selling Mike Walton thriller series. With Hunt Them Down, he starts a new series with protagonist Pierce Hunt, a former Army Ranger (RLTW) and current DEA special agent.

Hunt Them Down starts off with action and prejudice almost instantly. Action in the form of a DEA fire team prepping to take down a known drug cartel associate in Chicago and prejudice because along with this team, is a wet behind the ears, LEO hating, self-righteous reporter eager to win himself a Pulitzer prize. When this asshole reporter decides to live stream the entire thing from start to finish, the bad guys get wind of it, because this is 2019 and everyone is connected. When all is said and done, there is a dead DEA agent after a brutal shootout in the streets of Chicago. Our new protagonist, Pierce Hunt is one pissed off team leader, this anger and his actions gets him much respect from his LEO colleagues worldwide, but also suspended without pay for six months.

Once his suspension is up, he reports back for duty in a new city he was assigned, Miami. Meeting his new boss, it’s apparent his transition back to work was going to be a quick one. We learn that what made Pierce Hunt a star amongst his colleagues is his first successful undercover mission, which took two years to complete. It ruined Hunt’s real life and we learn later his work ruined other lives as well. His role or I should say, a way into the cartel as an undercover agent was through love. The end result was a known Cartel leader ending up in prison. This cartel leader decided to cut a deal with law enforcement and agreed to testify against others, basically using the DEA to do his dirty work for him and get rid of the competition. Pierce knows this, but the DEA needed a win and they would do anything they can to achieve some good press.

The other cartel leader, known as The Black Toska is vicious and blood thirsty with vengeance on her mind due to some brutality she was faced with as a young child. That vendetta and her aspirations to be the biggest drug smuggler, human trafficking advocate, even hinted at offering her services to middle eastern terrorists. Nothing is off limits with this cartel leader at the head of the table. Including kidnapping children in order to exact her revenge.

If getting ambushed, shot at, watching his fellow LEO members executed on the streets of Miami isn’t personal enough, things get really bad when The Black Toska forces Pierce to abandon all civilized manner and go on the hunt. Having made himself a promise long time ago that he would never let himself get that brutal again, describing his past and learning why he joined the DEA and separated from the Army, having gone against his command to rescue one of his teammates that had been taken as a POW. What we learn is that Hunt is as dedicated to his fellow team mates as he is to his family back home and when that family is threatened, absolutely nothing will stop Pierce from exacting revenge, rescuing those closest to him, and killing all the bad guys, which is described in spectacular vivid detail. The type of details only those authors who have lived that lifestyle have the capability to capture.

Simon Gervais brings a new series to the thriller community that is going to be an annual must read, Hunt Them Down is out in the wild and available in hardcover, e-book, and audio. Pick it up, you will not be able to put it down. I read it all in two sittings. Simon’s follow up, Trained to Hunt is available to pre-order and will be out, September 24th, 2019. After reading Hunt Them Down, I can’t wait to get my hands on his next Pierce Hunt novel.
