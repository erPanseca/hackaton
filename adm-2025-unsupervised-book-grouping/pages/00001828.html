Ricochet

Krista Ritchie, Becca Ritchie

As a sex addict, Lily Calloway must do the impossible. Stay celibate for 90 days.

Cravings and fantasies become her new routine, but while Loren Hale recovers from his alcohol addiction, Lily wonders if he’ll realize what a monster she really is. After all, her sexual compulsions begin to rule her life the longer she stays faithful to him.

Progress. That’s what Lily’s striving for. But by trying to become closer to her family—people who aren’t aware of her addiction—she creates larger obstacles. When she spends time with her youngest sister, she learns more about her than she ever imagined and senses an unsettling connection between Daisy and Ryke Meadows.

With Lily and Lo’s dysfunctional relationship teetering and unbalanced, they will need to find a way to reconnect from miles apart. But the inability to “touch” proves to be one of the hardest tests on their road to recovery. Some love brushes the surface. Some love is deeper than skin.

Lily and Lo have three months to discover just how deep their love really goes.

“You’re right. You don’t need a man, but you do need me.”
Okay but that’s literally the hottest statement I’ve ever read.

Lily and Loren are both working on their addictions, and I’m so happy that this wasn’t something that is being done quickly. The Ritchies did a great job in making this as realistic as possible.

Lily has let a lot more people into her life. I’m glad that almost everyone in her life is really there for her and is helping her in this journey. I especially enjoyed Rose’s involvement. She is a really good sister. It’s important to have these characters to highlight what kind of people one needs in their own life during a difficult time. I’m also glad that we delved deeper into the root of Lily and Lo’s addictions.

I also really liked her therapist!! She teaches Lily that she doesn’t need to completely stop having sex, but she needs to find a healthy balance. She also lets Lily know that she shouldn’t feel guilty for being this way. There is usually this guilt for some people having trouble with addictions or their mental health because they feel like they have “everything” they need and other people have way worse problems. This is always a great reminder for everyone.

Ryke’s character was really cool. From afar, he seems like a total jerk, but I surprisingly liked it. I just like how real he was. Sometimes we do need those characters who genuinely do not care to impress anyone. He really cares about Lo and Lily and does a great job in helping them get through these tough times.

I also like her sisters. They are all different and unique. I saw that Daisy and Rose have their own books, but I don’t know if I’m interested enough to read them.

I also really hope to get more of Lily and Lo. Their bond is just everything.

I’m hoping to read the next book soon!!! Also, the parents in this book suck!!

“I want to grow with him. I want to be with him. I want to love Lo without people telling me that our love is too much.”


This book broke my heart more than the first one did. Rereading it made me realise just how much deeper Lily and Loren’s addiction problems run and why it took them so long to disentangle themselves from their destructive and co-dependent relationship. They may not be the reason for their addictions, but they very well know how to enable each other. Still, I am so, so, so proud of them for finally getting the help they need and trying to deal with their addictions in a healthy way. Loren’s father and Lily’s mother played a huge role in their destructive addictions, and it hurts to see how these two rely on each other for all the love and affection that should have been given by their parents in the first place. I hate Jonathan, and I hate Samantha.

This book is centred mostly on Lily’s addiction and how and why they are so dependent on each other and their addictions. Ricochet is definitely better than the first one—not just because Lo is in rehab and Lily is getting therapy but also because of the many glimpses and scenes of their past as kids, teenagers, and adults. How Lo and Lily engraved each other into their lives to the point that one can’t live without the other.

Lily’s growth in this book was slow but so realistic. She did spiral a few times and had breakdowns because she felt alone, but I was glad she was making an effort to get more involved in her sister’s life and with her friends. She’s trying, even though she feels like she doesn’t deserve anything in her life. A few chapters about her past helped me understand her even better—why she has social anxiety but also hates being lonely. She’s been hurt and alone for years, but she just didn’t realise it until she went into therapy. Lily downplaying her sex addiction is one of the hardest things she has to overcome. She always thought that her family or friends wouldn’t understand her fears and urges just because she never had a valid reason or a triggering event that led her down this path. She tried to drown herself in sex because of loneliness, emptiness, and neglect—and her mother is solely responsible for it.

“Everyone eventually grows up. But if there’s anything I know for certain in this world—I never want to change without Loren Hale. We have to try to evolve together.”


Loren was in rehab for his addiction problems. I missed him so much in this book. I really wanted the Ritchie sisters to focus on his addiction while he was in rehab, but sadly, it wasn’t explored much. The flashbacks brought so much attention to his rebellious side and his need to be accepted and loved, which Jonathan exploited. He chose Lo only to criticise him and abandon him. How is it even possible that I am falling for this man on every page of this book? The things he did for Lily and to Lily had me absolutely swooning. The way he called her Love, the things he said to her, the way he reassured her so many times that he would always come back to her, the way he touched her, protected her since they were kids, made excuses to touch her under the pretence of their fake relationship—his I love yous and I miss yous—I love every broken and beautiful piece of Loren Hale.

“I love you,” he says again, “and no other man will ever say those words and mean them the way I do.”


Lily and Loren have been battling their addictions separately for three months now. They have never known how to live without each other, and it was heartbreaking to see them both so sad and in pain. Their fears and what ifs about their changed lives when Lo returns from rehab were so real, raw, and painful. They are both so scared of losing each other—what if the other realises that life is better without them? They have stopped enabling each other, and now they are working towards a healthier, more intimate relationship in which they don’t lie to their friends or family.

“Even if I make you miserable?”
There’s a long pause before he murmurs, “You don’t make me miserable. You make me want to live. And I want to live with you.”


Those few chapters about how Lo saved and protected Lily from the moment they became friends, how he fell first and fell harder for her, and how he would do absolutely anything to keep her at his side—I love them so much. I was a sobbing mess when they had their first conversation in weeks and when Lily felt so relieved that he called her. The first time she saw him on a video call had me sobbing with happy tears. Every interaction, every flashback, every conversation had me crying and smiling. I loved seeing how they have lived through the best and worst parts of their lives together, protecting each other because none of their parents were ready to accept them as they were.

“Our I love yous encompass years of heartache, of hurt, of laughter and pain. And every time we say the words, I feel the rush of our childhood. I couldn’t imagine ever losing that.”


Coballoway crumbs were everything! Their lively banter, their stupid 24-hour break-up over mundane things—they are just perfect. I loved the way Connor was there for Rose when her mother slapped her and how he assured her. Rose deserves so much better.

“I don’t need you,” she repeats, her voice breaking.
“You’re right,” he says softly. “You don’t need a man, Rose.” He pauses and I barely hear him whisper, “But you do need me.”

I get that Lo means a lot to him, but he can’t just tell Lily not to feel her emotions! I still feel like, if it weren’t for him, they would never be on a healthy path, but he seriously needs to take a chill pill.
