The battle on Sharr is over. The dark forest has fallen. Altair may be captive, but Zafira, Nasir, and Kifah are bound for Sultan’s Keep, determined to finish the plan he set in motion: restoring the hearts of the Sisters of Old to the minarets of each caliphate, and finally returning magic to all of Arawiya. But they are low on resources and allies alike, and the kingdom teems with fear of the Lion of the Night’s return.

As the zumra plots to overthrow the kingdom’s darkest threat, Nasir fights to command the magic in his blood. He must learn to hone his power into a weapon, to wield not only against the Lion but against his father, trapped under the Lion’s control. Zafira battles a very different darkness festering in her through her bond with the Jawarat—a darkness that hums with voices, pushing her to the brink of her sanity and to the edge of a chaos she dare not unleash. In spite of the darkness enclosing ever faster, Nasir and Zafira find themselves falling into a love they can’t stand to lose…but time is running out to achieve their ends, and if order is to be restored, drastic sacrifices will have to be made.

Lush and striking, hopeful and devastating, We Free the Stars is the masterful conclusion to the Sands of Arawiya duology by New York Times–bestselling author Hafsah Faizal.

Premise:

We Free the Stars is the sequel to We Hunt the Flame, following the zumra after as they continue on their quest to return magic to Arawiya.


- Initial Thoughts -

I am a husk of a human being. I don’t know how to move on after finishing this book.

(actually, I do know how to move on: read the book again... which I might have done)

We Hunt the Flame is one of my all-time favorite books. We Free the Stars had a lot to live up to, but I knew I would end up loving it. I did not expect how much. This book somehow managed to surpass all of my exceedingly high expectations and leave me an absolute mess.


- Writing & Setting -

We all already know that Hafsah Faizal’s writing is gorgeous. This is news to no one. But let me just say it again: the writing in this book is stunning, the prose is elegant without losing a sense of humor, and the setting is fascinating. We got to know Arawiya a little bit in the first book, but We Free the Stars shows us more of this world and made me never want to leave.


- Plot -

This book continues on the quest to restore magic and save Arawiya from the danger that was unleashed at the end of We Hunt the Flame (I’m trying to be vague here for anyone who hasn’t read the first one yet).

Where it took me a minute to get into the first book, I was immediately hooked in We Free the Stars. There was something constantly happening or about to happen. There was also a decent amount of action in this book, which is always fun, while still having time to let the fantastic characters shine.


- Characters -

Speaking of those fantastic characters: I love the zumra with every fibre of my being.

The characters in this book are amazing. You thought you loved them after We Hunt the Flame? Just you wait.

We get three POVs in this book: Zafira, Nasir, and Altair.

I loved the addition of Altair’s perspective. He became one of my favorite characters in We Hunt the Flame and I was so happy to see more of him in We Free the Stars (even though I was constantly worrying about him). Learning more about him was one of my favorite parts of this book.

Nasir and Zafira’s perspectives are equally amazing. They both have gone through a lot in book one and it was great to see them continue to develop and change over the course of this book.

We Free the Stars also puts a good amount of focus on the romance that began in We Hunt the Flame and it was just so perfect. I honestly don’t know how to put into words my feelings over that plotline because my thoughts are still an incoherent mess whenever I try to think of it.

Outside of our POV characters, we see some familiar faces and some new ones. I loved spending even more time with the characters we already knew from We Hunt the Flame, and I also really enjoyed getting to know all of the new characters. There was not a single flat character in this series and I love it so much.


- Conclusion -

Pros- Beautiful writing, fantastic characters, swoon-worthy romance, amazing worldbuilding, addictive plot
Cons- I don’t know what to do with my life now that this series is over. Also, this book made me cry in the middle of a livestream
Overall- 5/5 stars.
I could not have imagined a more perfect finale. This book was everything I could have ever wanted it to be.

ㅤㅤㅤ “Darkness doesn’t need to be destroyed. We need the dark as much as we need light. It makes us bold, as much as it makes us afraid.”

ㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤ ㅤㅤㅤ ━ 4.3 - 4.5 ☆ ━
ㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤ⊱ ──── might contain spoilers ────⊰

ㅤㅤOne thing I can tell you about this book is that this is by far the biggest improvement I’ve ever seen in a duology. This book brought absolutely everything that the first one failed to acquire and more. I am not sure if I just simply ended up becoming slightly biased, but if a book manages to make me fall in love with its characters and get attached to them in such a way that I come to relate to their feelings and experiences, for me, that is a good book. And this is exactly what this book delivered beautifully.

ㅤㅤ"We Free the Stars" is the second and final book in the Sands of Arawiya duology and, my lord, what a beautiful book it is and what an amazing and satisfying conclusion it was for this rather interesting and fascinating adventure. As expected, the journey to free the old magic could not be that easy or that fast to end. The "zumra", now short of two very important and impressive members, is faced with an even greater danger they have to overcome: the Lion is nowhere near giving up and may be more dangerous than ever in his hungry quest for power. But don't worry, a very intriguing, wise and full of magic book is here to help, and with both old and new allies, nothing can stop the courageous Huntress and her faithful cadre from reaching their original purpose, even while separated. So, before embarking on this adventure, prepare yourself for heartbreak, innocent and sparkling love, and some very intriguing characters that you will find yourself falling for more and more.

ㅤㅤ Now, as always, I am going to just develop a bit more on these aspects below and hope I will find enough words to transcribe my thoughts.


ㅤㅤㅤㅤ -ˋˏ ༻ world-building, writing and action ༺ ˎˊ-

ㅤㅤCompared to the previous book, there isn't much to offer in terms of world-building. Almost all of the world's details were previously provided in the first book, so the attention in this one shifts to the action. I did, however, love how the book allows the reader to finally see more inside the caliphates and have a somewhat greater sense of how varied these five places are, despite their physical proximity. I would have liked a few more details at times, but given the events of the book and how the action was portrayed, I believe it was done well enough.

ㅤㅤIn terms of the writing, my major issue for the first book was the excessive use of cultural components and names for various things that were not appropriately addressed and clarified. I enjoyed seeing this book resolve that issue and reduce that massive quantity of material in such a way that it became less tiresome. And it managed all of this while maintaining the book's unique air and greatly influencing cultural themes, which I thought was fantastic. The language in this book is amazing, and I found myself repeating individual paragraphs and sentences more than once because they were written so well.  I enjoyed the wordcraft in this book, as well as all of the accolades for the author.

ㅤㅤUnlike the first book, in which the majority of the action takes place on the island of Sharr, the protagonists in this book begin to wander across the fictitious world of Arawiya in their effort to correct prior mistakes and achieve their objective. The speed of the action picks up significantly, and you're quickly swept up in the fighting and story development. If I am completely honest and decide to judge this book more harshly, I will still say that the first part of the book is a little slow, but it is completely understandable, and I believe it was the right decision to approach this plot because the characters needed time to get back on their feet and prepare for what comes next.


ㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤ -ˋˏ ༻ characters ༺ ˎˊ-

ㅤㅤUnlike the previous book, when my opinions about the characters were not as positive, this second book definitely managed to make me become so connected to some of these characters that I began relating to and loving them in a specific way.

ㅤㅤ ✧ Zafira - what a great development our favourite tall, broad, and powerful female main character finally gets. Unlike the first book where she is this very closed and stoic character, Zafira gets so much closer to the reader in this book and the reader slowly finds out more about her feelings, gets deeper into her mind and sees what exactly is the reason behind her blockade when it comes to feelings. She goes through so much only to do the right thing, and she consistently sacrifices everything, making her one of the most selfless people ever. She isn't one of my favorite characters, for sure, but she is actually well-suited to the duology. You will be unable to avoid falling for her and her uncomfortable, awkward, yet charming exchanges.

ㅤㅤ ✧ Nasir (the shy rizzer!!!) - I'd been captivated by this character since the first book, so you can imagine how fast I fell in love with him after he received the appropriate growth and attention from the writer. This character is a complete contradiction; you will blush at his directness and wicked ways of thinking, but you will also experience second-hand embarrassment when he is forced to share those sentiments and ideas. He is simply the result of so much pain, suffering, and trauma that your heart will simply soften each time you have to watch him truly try to overcome his limits and accept himself to the point where he will be able to see himself as a worthy person.

ㅤㅤ ❧ Oh, how beautifully this love story develops, yet it still needed slightly more for it to truly become perfect. I just loved how purely and slowly this love was developing between the two main characters and it is simply so suitable for them. These are two characters that both need to find themselves first and they do it so beautifully together that they only end up realising how significant the other is for them in the most perfectly placed moments. However….please, please just give these two a room and some privacy away from everyone else…they just NEED IT BALDY because poor them keep getting interrupted at all times and Nasir just wastes his little wild rizz on nothing 😭

ㅤㅤ ✧ Altair - Still one of my favourite characters in this book, and I'm overjoyed with his conclusion and how he finally got what he deserved. He is such a selfless and brilliant character that it is only fitting that he becomes such an important element of the tale and he gets raised to main character status. His points of view were both sad and beautiful, and they allow you to see so much more beyond the jokester persona that he is putting on for everyone's sake. Nothing made me happier than his tight bond with Nasir, since I am always drawn to this sort of pure affection between camarades. Also I might have guessed a little about the outcome ever since my first review without even acknowledging it, but I will not say what.

ㅤㅤ ✧ Kifah - The ideal, ruthless, yet hilarious individual who completes this group in the most appropriate way. It felt nice to finally learn more about this character and her past, and it was wonderful to witness her and Zafira's relationship bloom. It was just heartwarming, and it was most likely exactly what they needed at the time.

ㅤㅤ ✧ The Jawarat 📖 - I am sorry, but I will just proceed and mention a book as a character, once you read this, you will fully understand why, and I might just say this has easily became my favourite character. Yes, you heard that right 🙌🏻



ㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤㅤ -ˋˏ ༻ ending and conclusions ༺ ˎˊ-

ㅤㅤThe finale was arguably the best for the entire duology, and it will undoubtedly make you grin as you finish this book. For me, it may have been significantly better handled if it had focused more on the individuals and their new lives, as well as the world's new structure. Overall, a satisfactory finale with a lovely end and a right conclusion for these characters, while I may simply be a little harsh and too hard to totally please.

ㅤㅤFinally, I'd want to state unequivocally that you cannot adequately appraise a series of novels based just on the first volume. What may begin as a bit of negative narrative can turn into a really beautiful story that will occupy your time with wonderful feelings, imagery, and yet another great journey to remember. "We Free the Stars" is absolutely one of the novels capable of attaining this, and it achieves it very wonderfully. I would recommend to have some patience, enjoy an eastern imagery, and give this duology a try if you ever feel like reading something easy and pretty enjoyable.

THE RELIGIOUS APPROPRIATION/RAMPANT CONTRADICTIONS:

For some reason, in this book, random buildings have 'minarets', like minarets are some Arab version of a tower block or a clock tower or towers of a medieval fortress. Here is a basic definition of a MINARET on Google:

Minaret, (Arabic: “beacon”) in Islamic religious architecture, the tower from which the faithful are called to prayer five times each day by a muezzin, or crier. Such a tower is always connected with a mosque and has one or more balconies or open galleries.

Minarets are exclusive to masjids and the Islamic faith. This is the equivalent of sticking a Buddhist vihara in front of a club, or on a government building.

Now armed with a mind-to-mind connection to a powerful magical text and outed as a Huntress, not a Hunter, she's struggling to adjust to her new situation amidst the panic of their quest. Oh, and there's the exciting (or distracting?) feelings she's experiencing for the crown prince, Nasir.

Nasir, the crown prince of Arawiya and the famed assassin known as the Prince of Death, is also dealing with some shattering revelations. Having just found out that he has a brother—and discovering that the brother is his commander at arms and lifelong reluctant frenemy, Altair—is enough to make him stop in his tracks. But then to discover that his father, the evil Sultan, is also under the Lion's mind control and therefore not the monster Nasir believed him to be for years? Yikes. Nasir is, to put it mildly, a bit of an emotional mess and attempting to hide it. And there's also the fated pull he feels for Zafira too, in case he didn't have enough going on.

With court politics, assassinations, intrigue, and deadly games of cat and mouse to come, We Free the Stars takes off with a lot on its plate. Will Arawiya be saved?

My thoughts:
So I need to address the elephant in the room right off the bat: the pacing of this novel really suffered with the extreme length of this book. Even though it was only roughly 100 pages longer than the first book, this installment felt every inch of its extra page count.

I think this was a difficult series to wrap up, honestly, and it speaks to the author's talented sentences and character development that I still loved it... even when it dragged on. And it did drag. Part of what made me fixate on the length was the somewhat aimless portion around the 250-350 page mark where I felt like the characters were all aimlessly pacing from space to space, waiting for the shoe to drop and filling the time with movement to feel productive. That sounds super dramatic—but I feel like it's accurate. I enjoyed those portions for the conversations and the romantic angst, but even my "character drama"-preferring self was ready for some action after a while.

However, despite those qualms above, I really did love this book.

Hafsah Faizal is a beautiful writer and I fell in love with her characters and this world. Nasir and Zafira's romance stands alone in my head for its refreshing blend of good "old fashioned" YA angst and drama mixed with a sensual edge that didn't rely on raw sexual insta-lust to make it work. I was also a huge fan of the side characters and their unique emotional arcs.

☆ 5 / 5 ☆

We Hunt the Flame: 5 / 5

“We were not born to fight, but our cradles were built from struggles and hardships. Pens, swords, sticks- weapons shoved into our fists as soon as we’re old enough to grasp them. So we fight, because the world will cut our throats otherwise.”

I have completely and utterly fallen in love with Hafsah Faizal’s writing and storytelling. The Sands of Arawiya duology has truly given me such joy and I can’t believe it took me this long to get to it. I can however say that this story and these characters will be staying with me a long time.

This installment picks up where we left off, with the crew sailing back to Sultan’s Keep on one side, and the Lion and Altair on the other. We meet more safins of the HIgh Circle, allies to the restoration of magic to Arawiya. However, cooperation isn’t always easy and old wounds keep being reopened.

“She was the bladed compass, honed by the Lion and wielded by the Jawarat. She was ruin, she was havoc, and she reveled in it.”

After the strife Zafira has encountered on Shaar, it should be a relief for her to head back to Arawiya and the people she’s known all her life. However, without the Arz, without magic, without the Lion and his grooming, with her village decimated and with such a heavy heart, she doesn’t know who she can be. While thrilled to be by her sister’s side once again, she slowly comes to terms with Lana growing up without her realising and that she was never quite as sweet or innocent as she remembered her. I loved seeing their interactions so much, seeing how much they care for one another, and Lana with her unrestrained will to help the wounded and angry thoughts really stole the show for me!

Zafira is also reluctant to see Yasmine again, to explain to her how her brother died, unable to find the words to bring her that pain and sorrow. So she finds a strange sort of comfort in her bond with the Jawarat, despite its obvious desire for chaos and control over her. It gradually indulges in her anger, in her pain and loss, in what she could do if she had magic again. While she tries to fight against its most murderous intents, it becomes harder for her heart to not seethe with righteous violence.

“Somewhere on Shaar his heart had found a beat, and he intended to keep it going.”

Nasir has to deal with his own ghosts and has to come to a decision where it comes to his father. He furiously believes that breaking the medallion will break the Lion’s control over him and bring back the kind man he once knew. But abuse leaves undeniable traces and even with this little bit of hope in his heart, he doesn’t know how he can reconcile the face of his father with love. Nasir still despises the expectations that come with him being the Prince, and the cost and burden that power comes with. His shadow powers are difficult to control and his emotions are piling up to be too much to rail in. He’s definitely more open in this book than he was in the first one but Altair’s absence weighs on him more than he’d like to admit.

Altair himself has to wrestle with the new knowledge that the Lion is his father and that all his plotting might’ve been in vain. I absolutely loved having some chapters from his perspective here, showing a more vulnerable side of him. It was especially interesting to see his true feelings for both his parents, how the shame his mother felt about herself was projected onto him, and how he slowly finds empathy for the Lion. It’s a dangerous path but one he can’t help taking under the influence of his father’s mind games.

“You seem to have no trouble being the end of me.”

The plot may keep the members of the zumra apart at times, but when they come back together it is with so much heart, even if it could rip yours to shreds. I love their dynamic as a group but also how they work in pairs. Obviously Zafira and Nasir’s relationship gives me all the angst and moments wrought with yearning I could ever want with a tension few can write as well as Hafsah Faizal. Nasir truly had some beautiful lines in this book but it’s all the unsaid words and almost moments that hold me in their clutches. My heart truly sings with joy every time we get to have Altair and Kifah on page together. They have such a perfect dynamic that can’t help but make you smile and laugh even in the most desperate of moments.

“Do you understand? You’ll have me. No matter how thick the night, I will always be there to light your way.”

All characters grapple with grief and their identity in this book, with little time to think about themselves after this war and the author never forgets to give more dimension to a conflict that would otherwise seem too straightforward. The ending was so perfect and bittersweet, giving us everything we could want but still leaving the lives of these characters a little imperfect. I’ve honestly been blown away by this series and I can’t wait to read more works from this author and discover all the worlds they have to offer.
